## 1. Eins (Sentence 1)

> [!quote] Transcription by Wilkins
> “Meinen Dank für die Auszeichnung, welche mir die Akademie durch die Aufnahme unter ihre Correspondenten hat zu Theil werden lassen, **glaube ich am besten dadurch zu erkennen zu geben**, dass ich von der hierdurch erhaltenen Erlaubniss baldigst Gebrauch mache durch Mittheilung einer Untersuchung über die Häufigkeit der Primzahlen; ein Gegenstand, welcher durch die Interesse, welches Gauss und Dirichlet demselben längere Zeit geschenkt haben, einer solchen Mitteilung vielleicht nicht ganz unwerth erscheint.”

> [!abstract] A Very Honest Translation by Arkytoddler
> My thanks for the award which the academy has bestowed upon me by admitting me among their correspondents, I believe, is best acknowledged by promptly making use of the permission thereby received to share a study on the frequency* of prime numbers - a subject which, due to the interest that Gauss and Dirichlet have themselves long devoted to it, may seem not entirely unworthy of such communication.
> 
> *\*Sometimes called accumulation*

### I. THE LANGUAGE

<details>

Written in 1859, this opening paragraph, comprised of one sentence with no full stop, reflects a style not uncommon in old-fashioned and academic writings in then Germany, where the creation of extensive yet grammatically correct compound sentences, enabled by the flexible sentence structure that includes the use of multiple clauses, subclauses, and relative clauses connected by conjunctions and commas, a feature shared by many other languages such as Latin, Greek, and English, was employed both to explore complex ideas without breaking the syntactic flow, as demonstrated by this paragraph itself, and to convey a specific mood or mimic the unstructured flow of thoughts, as seen in Thomas Mann’s novels.  (Please see Joyce’s *Ulysses*). 

The main idea clause has been bolded in the German block above: *"...glaube ich am besten dadurch zu erkennen zu geben..."*.

</details>

### II. THE HISTORY

<details>

Die Akademie (the Academy) mentioned here refers to the Berlin Academy of Sciences, initially known as the Prussian Academy of Sciences. Founded in 1700 by Elector Frederick III of Brandenburg, who would later become King Frederick I of Prussia, it was a pure-research establishment modeled on England’s Royal Society. In Germany then, the distinction between universities and academies or societies was significant. While the universities were responsible for the training and preparation of professionals—such as teachers, physicians, lawyers, and preachers—the academy existed solely for the purpose of research.

The Berlin Academy soon became one of the most prestigious scientific institutions of the time, playing a crucial role in the dissemination of scientific works. Over the years, the Berlin Academy has been associated with many distinguished scientists and intellectuals, including Bernhard Riemann.

1859 was a great year for Bernhard Riemann. The 32-year-old mathematician was finally promoted to full professor at Göttingen, which secured him a stable income. What was more, on August 11, shortly before turning 33, Riemann was appointed a corresponding member of the Berlin Academy of Sciences (die Aufnahme unter ihre Correspondenten)—a prestigious honor, especially for a young mathematician who had only published two well-known papers: his 1851 doctoral dissertation and his 1857 work on Abelian functions.

As per the tradition of the Academy, new members were expected to submit an original paper presenting their current research as a form of acknowledgment of their appointment. Riemann’s submission in response to this tradition was his paper on the distribution of prime numbers—the very work we are exploring here.

Born in 1777, Carl Friedrich Gauss, also known as the "Prince of Mathematicians," laid the foundation of modern number theory in his 1801 seminal work, *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae*, in which he transformed a scattered collection of isolated theorems into a rigorous, systematic discipline. However, his work on the frequency of prime numbers, one of his most famous contributions to the field, occurs largely in the margins of his personal logs and correspondence.

In 1792, still a teenager, Gauss began examining tables of prime numbers to see how they were distributed. He noticed that as numbers get larger, primes become "thinner" or less frequent in a predictable way. He conjectured and arrived at what would later be known as $Li(x)$, which provides an incredibly accurate estimation of prime density:

$$Li(x) = \int_{2}^{x} \frac{dt}{\ln t}$$

Gauss never published a formal proof of this conjecture. It took another century before Jacques Hadamard and Charles Jean de la Vallée Poussin independently proved the Prime Number Theorem in 1896 using complex analysis. And of course, by then, Bernhard Riemann, the author of the paper, had been dead for 30 years.

Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet, yet another German, picked up where Gauss left off and provided the analytical rigor needed to turn conjectures about prime frequency into provable theorems. His work is the bridge between the classical number theory of the *Disquisitiones* and modern analytic number theory.

Dirichlet’s most famous contribution is his Theorem on Arithmetic Progressions, published in 1837 in the *Abhandlungen der Königlichen Preußischen Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin*—the same academy mentioned above, which Herr Riemann later joined. His paper title was:

“Beweis des Satzes, dass jede unbegrenzte arithmetische Progression, deren erstes Glied und Differenz ganze Zahlen ohne gemeinschaftlichen Factor sind, unendlich viele Primzahlen enthält.”

Translated into English: "Proof of the theorem that every unbounded arithmetic progression, whose first term and difference are integers without a common factor, contains infinitely many prime numbers." This specific memoir is widely recognized by historians as the official birth of analytic number theory.

Dirichlet had a profound respect for Gauss, famously keeping a copy of Gauss’s *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* by his side throughout his life. In this 1837 paper, he essentially solved a problem that Gauss had acknowledged as a conjecture by Legendre but hadn't yet proven.

Later in 1839, Dirichlet published a second, "simplified" version of the proof in *Crelle's Journal* (*Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik*), which is often the version modern students encounter because it refined the use of the $L$-series characters.

The relationship between Gauss, Dirichlet, and Riemann is one of the most significant "genealogies" in mathematical history. They represent three consecutive generations of the Göttingen school, where the torch of number theory was passed from one to the other, evolving from observation to analysis.

In 1855, when Gauss passed away, Dirichlet was the natural choice to succeed him as the Chair of Higher Mathematics at the University of Göttingen. He essentially took over the "throne" of German mathematics. Riemann was a student at Göttingen when Dirichlet arrived to take Gauss's chair. Dirichlet recognized Riemann's genius early on and became his primary mentor.

The connection between these three mathematicians, the Academy (specifically the Berlin Academy of Sciences), and the University of Göttingen defines the golden age of German mathematics. Their relationship was not just intellectual, but institutional, involving a strategic "brain drain" between Berlin and Göttingen.

### III. THE MATHEMATICS

<details>

In this session, we are going to have a brief look at the Prime Numbers and Gauss’s Prime Number Conjecture. Before that, we need an official definition for the prime numbers:

**Die Primzahlen (Prime Numbers)** are natural numbers greater than 1, divisible only by 1 and itself. 

Natural numbers are positive integers used for counting, such as 1, 2, 3, etc. Depending on the specific mathematical context, some natural numbers also include zero. $1$ is not considered a prime number as it does not satisfy the formal definition of a prime number that it has exactly two distinct positive divisors.

Gauss's prime number conjecture, now known as the **Prime Number Theorem**, states that the number of prime numbers up to a given bound (denoted as $x$) can be accurately approximated by the logarithmic integral:

$$\pi(x) \approx Li(x) = \int_{2}^{x} \frac{dt}{\ln t}$$

* **$x$**: The bound, the independent variable, and the upper limit of the evaluation. If you want to estimate how many primes exist below 1,000,000, then $x = 1,000,000$.
* **$\pi(x)$**: The far left-hand side represents the Prime Counting Function. It counts the exact number of primes less than or equal to $x$. Because primes are integers, the graph of $\pi(x)$ is a jagged staircase that flatlines and then abruptly jumps up by exactly 1 every time it hits a prime number.
* **$Li$**: In the middle, $Li$ is the functional operator for the Logarithmic Integral (derived from the German *Logarithmische Integral*). It is not a variable, but the name of the mathematical function itself.
* **$\int_{2}^{x} \frac{dt}{\ln t}$**: The right-hand side is the logarithmic integral itself. 
* **$\frac{1}{\ln t}$**: The Density Fraction represents the height of the curve at any given point $t$, which mirrors the physical probability of a number being prime. For example, around the number 100, roughly 1 in every $\ln(100)$ numbers is prime (~0.217). Around the number 1,000,000, the density drops to 1 in every $\ln(1,000,000)$ numbers (~0.072).

Therefore, the frequency of prime numbers gets thinner and thinner. 

Geometrically, the integral acts like an automated, continuous summing machine. Instead of adding numbers one by one, it calculates the cumulative area under the curve $\frac{1}{\ln t}$ from $t = 2$ to $t = x$.

The following is a table summarising a comparison of $\pi(x)$ and $Li(x)$:

| **$10^1$** | 4 | 5.12 | +1.12 | 28.00000% | 1.2800000 |

| **$10^3$** | 168 | 176.56 | +8.56 | 5.09524% | 1.0509524 |
| **$10^4$** | 1,229 | 1,245.09 | +16.09 | 1.30919% | 1.0130919 |
| **$10^5$** | 9,592 | 9,628.80 | +36.80 | 0.38365% | 1.0038365 |
| **$10^6$** | 78,498 | 78,626.50 | +128.50 | 0.16370% | 1.0016370 |

The table shows a consistent trend of overestimate by $Li(x)$. However, J.E. Littlewood famously proved in 1914 that the two functions cross paths and change leads infinitely many times, though the first crossing happens at an unimaginably large number (Skewes' number).

Even though the absolute number of missed primes grows, the percentage error drops precipitously, dipping below $0.001\%$ by $10^{10}$. The value perfectly illustrates the asymptotic behavior defined by the Prime Number Theorem.

## 2. Zwei (Sentence 2)

The second sentence goes straight to the theme:

> [!quote] Original Text
> “Bei dieser Untersuchung diente mir als Ausgangspunkt die von Euler gemachte Bemerkung, dass das Product
> $$\prod \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}} = \sum n^{-s}$$
> wenn für $p$ alle Primzahlen, für $n$ alle ganzen Zahlen gesetzt werden.”

> [!abstract] David R. Wilkins Translation (Preliminary Version 1998)
> With this investigation my point of departure is provided by the observation of Euler that the product
> $$\prod \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}} = \sum n^{-s}$$
> if one substitutes for $p$ all prime numbers, and for $n$ all whole numbers.

### I. THE LANGUAGE

<details>

#### Word Order

Although both German and English belong to the West Germanic language branch and share deeply embedded similarities in their vocabularies, they evolved highly divergent syntactic structures centuries ago, primarily driven by how they handle verbs and noun cases. While modern English relies on a rigid Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) template to maintain clarity after losing its case system, German uses flexible noun positioning bound by strict verbal constraints.

Let’s examine the differences together. First, we strip off all the decorations and get to the essential German core sentence in the main clause:

* **German:** <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**Die Bemerkung**</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">**diente**</span> <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**mir**</span> <span style="color:#4a5568">**als Ausgangspunkt**</span>.
* **English:** <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**The observation**</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">**served**</span> <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**to me**</span> <span style="color:#4a5568">**as the departure point**</span>.

They both follow the same basic sentence structure: 
* <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**Subject**</span> – <span style="color:#b7791f">**Verb**</span> – <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**Dative Object**</span> – <span style="color:#4a5568">**Prepositional Complement**</span>

However, when other descriptive or specifying phrases and conditions are added, things change:

**In German:**
* <span style="color:#dd6b20">**Bei dieser Untersuchung**</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">**diente**</span> <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**mir**</span> <span style="color:#4a5568">**als Ausgangspunkt**</span> <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**die von Euler gemachte Bemerkung**</span>.<br>
(<span style="color:#dd6b20">**Context**</span> – <span style="color:#b7791f">**Verb**</span> – <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**Dative Object**</span> – <span style="color:#4a5568">**Prepositional Complement**</span> – <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**Subject**</span>)

**A faithful yet grammatically correct English translation:**
* <span style="color:#dd6b20">**With this investigation**</span>, <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**the observation made by Euler**</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">**served**</span> <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**to me**</span> <span style="color:#4a5568">**as the departure point**</span>.<br>
(<span style="color:#dd6b20">**Context**</span>, <span style="color:#2b6cb0">**Subject**</span> – <span style="color:#b7791f">**Verb**</span> – <span style="color:#2c7a7b">**Dative Object**</span> – <span style="color:#4a5568">**Prepositional Complement**</span>)

One can see that in the original German sentence, the subject **"die Bemerkung"** (the observation) has been pushed from the start to the end following the addition of the context **"Bei dieser Untersuchung"** (with this investigation), which Riemann put in the **First Position (P1)**.

This "subject inversion" occurs because German strictly mandates that the inflected verb must sit in the **Second Position (V2)**. The verb **"diente"** immediately follows P1, forcing the true subject (**"die... Bemerkung"**) to the very end of the clause. This is possible because the article **"die"** in **"die... Bemerkung"** acts as a morphological tag, telling the reader: *"No matter where I am sitting in this sentence, I am the Nominative Subject."*

English syntax, on the other hand, resists this. A literal translation that preserves the German sentence structure with only verbatim replacement would be: *“With this investigation served to me as the departure point the by-Euler-made observation.”* This does not make sense because modern English has shed the case endings that clarify that the observation is the entity doing the serving, relying entirely on rigid word order to signal meaning.

The cases in the German language guarantee immense flexibility in the positioning of nouns. For example, besides what Riemann wrote, there are several other word orders that make the sentence correct and sound, though each carries a different emphasis:

1.  **<span style="color:#2b6cb0">Subject</span> – <span style="color:#b7791f">Verb</span> – <span style="color:#2c7a7b">Dative Object</span> – <span style="color:#dd6b20">Context</span> – <span style="color:#4a5568">Prepositional Complement</span>**<br>
    <span style="color:#2b6cb0">Die von Euler gemachte Bemerkung</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">diente</span> <span style="color:#2c7a7b">mir</span> <span style="color:#dd6b20">bei dieser Untersuchung</span> <span style="color:#4a5568">als Ausgangspunkt</span>.
2.  **<span style="color:#2c7a7b">Dative Object</span> – <span style="color:#b7791f">Verb</span> – <span style="color:#2b6cb0">Subject</span> – <span style="color:#dd6b20">Context</span> – <span style="color:#4a5568">Prepositional Complement</span>**<br>
    <span style="color:#2c7a7b">Mir</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">diente</span> <span style="color:#2b6cb0">die von Euler gemachte Bemerkung</span> <span style="color:#dd6b20">bei dieser Untersuchung</span> <span style="color:#4a5568">als Ausgangspunkt</span>.
3.  **<span style="color:#4a5568">Prepositional Complement</span> – <span style="color:#b7791f">Verb</span> – <span style="color:#2c7a7b">Dative Object</span> – <span style="color:#dd6b20">Context</span> – <span style="color:#2b6cb0">Subject</span>**<br>
    <span style="color:#4a5568">Als Ausgangspunkt</span> <span style="color:#b7791f">diente</span> <span style="color:#2c7a7b">mir</span> <span style="color:#dd6b20">bei dieser Untersuchung</span> <span style="color:#2b6cb0">die von Euler gemachte Bemerkung</span>.

You might have noticed that, besides the rule that the inflected verb must occupy position 2, the dative object **"mir"** always follows the verb closely. This is the **German Pronoun Rule**. In the middle field (*Mittelfeld*) of a German sentence, unstressed personal pronouns (like *mir*) are incredibly light. By grammatical law, they must migrate to the absolute left—directly after the inflected verb.

#### The Modifier

German possesses a unique ability to pack dense, clause-like descriptions right in front of a noun like a giant adjective. Riemann writes **"die [von Euler gemachte] Bemerkung"** (literally: *"the [by Euler made] observation"*). English is unnatural with this "middle-loading" syntax and is forced to unravel the structure into a trailing, post-nominal relative phrase: **"the observation [made by Euler]"**.

#### The Subordinate Clause

In the final dependent clause introduced by the conjunction *dass*: 

> “...dass das Product $\prod \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}} = \sum n^{-s}$, wenn für $p$ alle Primzahlen, für $n$ alle ganzen Zahlen gesetzt werden.”

German shifts from its V2 main-clause layout to a strict **Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)** layout inside dependent clauses. It clusters the arguments in the middle and ejects the entire passive verb complex—**"gesetzt werden"**—to the absolute end of the sentence. English, conversely, maintains its predictable SVO alignment inside sub-clauses, placing the verb immediately after the actor (*"...if one substitutes..."*).

Additionally, German syntax treats subordinate clauses like isolated grammatical islands. By law, every single subordinate clause must be separated from its surrounding clauses by a hard comma boundary (in this case, anchoring right before the conjunction *dass*).

</details>
